Yes, it is primarily due to the physical shape of console characters.

While the canvas dimensions (60x20) determine the resolution of your grid, the oval shape is caused by the aspect ratio of terminal fonts.

The Cause: Rectangular Character Cells
In standard text-based console terminals (like CMD, PowerShell, or bash), character cells are not square. They are vertical rectangles—usually about 1.8 to 2 times taller than they are wide.

If you draw a circle using a standard 1:1 mathematical formula (e.g., 10 columns wide by 10 rows high), it will appear on your screen as a vertical oval that is twice as tall as it is wide.

How the Code Compensates
To correct for this, our circle drawing algorithm in 
canvas.c
 dynamically stretches the circle horizontally by a scaling factor of 1.8:

c


int x = (int)(cx + r * cos(theta) * 1.8 + 0.5);
int y = (int)(cy + r * sin(theta) + 0.5);
This means a circle of radius 5 actually spans 19 columns horizontally but only 11 rows vertically, which should look circular on most default console fonts.

How to tune it for your screen
Because different terminals use different fonts (Consolas, Courier New, MS Gothic) and line-spacing heights, you may need to tweak this factor slightly to get a perfect circle on your specific screen:

If the circle looks like a vertical oval (too tall): The terminal characters are taller than expected. We should increase the scale factor in canvas.c to 2.0 or 2.1.

If the circle looks like a horizontal oval (too wide): The terminal characters are wider than expected. We should decrease the scale factor in canvas.c to 1.5 or 1.6.

why does my line look broken when I draw it at a steep angle?
8:09 PM, 6/10/2026


This is a very common visual effect in text-based graphics, and it is caused by three factors: low grid resolution, the rectangular shape of console characters, and diagonal pixel connectivity.

Here is why it happens:

1. Diagonal Connectivity (Corner-to-Corner Jumps)
Bresenham's line algorithm guarantees 8-way connectivity. This means that consecutive pixels touch either at their flat edges (up, down, left, right) or only at their corners (diagonally). At steep angles (like 45° to 85°), consecutive asterisks are plotted diagonally from each other:

text


      *
    *
  *
In a standard font, each text character cell has space (padding) around the asterisk (*) glyph. When two asterisks are adjacent only by their corners, they do not physically touch on the screen. This corner-only connection creates gaps, making the line look like a chain of dotted steps rather than a solid line.

2. Tall Console Characters (The 2:1 Aspect Ratio)
As we discussed with the circle, console characters are about twice as tall as they are wide. When a line steps 1 unit vertically, that step is visually twice as large as a 1-unit horizontal step. This stretches the vertical gaps between your asterisks, making the diagonal "staircase" steps look much taller and more disjointed than they would on a square pixel display.

3. Coarse Grid Resolution
Because the canvas is relatively small (60x20), each character cell is a large block. On high-resolution pixel displays, these steps are tiny and smoothed out (often using anti-aliasing). On a low-resolution text canvas, the steps are large and highly visible.

What you can do:
This is a physical limitation of ASCII art/terminal graphics, but you can improve the visual continuity by:

Using gentler angles: Lines that are closer to horizontal (more columns than rows) or perfectly vertical/horizontal will have cells sharing flat edges, which makes them look completely solid.
Using a different character: Sometimes characters like # (hash) or █ (full block, if your console page supports UTF-8) fill more of the cell space and touch at the corners better than the thin asterisk * glyph.
